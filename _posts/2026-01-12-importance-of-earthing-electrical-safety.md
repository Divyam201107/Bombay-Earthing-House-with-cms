Every electrical installation relies on one essential system to keep people and equipment safe: earthing. A properly designed earthing system provides a low-resistance path for fault current and lightning discharge to flow harmlessly into the ground.

## What earthing actually does

When a fault occurs — a damaged cable, short circuit, or lightning strike — current looks for the path of least resistance. Without a properly designed earthing system, that path can be through building structures, metal piping, or a person in contact with faulty equipment.

## The role of the earth electrode

The earth electrode is the component buried in soil that maintains a consistently low resistance to ground across seasons and soil conditions. Copper bonded earth rods are favoured for critical installations because copper offers the lowest resistivity of any commercially used earthing metal.

## Why resistance value matters

Earthing systems are designed to a target resistance — often below 1 to 5 ohms per IS 3043. Higher resistance means fault current takes longer to dissipate, increasing risk to equipment and personnel.

## Earthing and lightning protection work together

A lightning arrester intercepts a strike at the air terminal, but it is the down conductor and earth electrode that determine whether that energy is dissipated safely. Both should always be specified as a single coordinated system.

## Key takeaways

- Earthing protects people and equipment by giving fault current a safe path to ground.
- Electrode material — copper bonded, GI pipe — should match soil conditions and installation criticality.
- Target resistance values should be verified through testing, not assumed.
- Periodic testing before monsoon season keeps the system performing as designed.
